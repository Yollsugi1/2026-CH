# A-B-C Framework: Detailed Guide

The Yoon Sung-won A-B-C Framework is Creatorhood's core content planning methodology. This guide provides deep examples and exercises.

## Philosophy

Every compelling piece of content answers three questions:
1. **Who** is doing something? (A)
2. **What** are they doing? (B)
3. **So what?** Why should I care? (C)

The magic happens when A and B are calibrated correctly:
- If both A and B are ordinary → boring
- If both A and B are extraordinary → disconnected from reality
- **If ONE grounds it and ONE elevates it → compelling**

---

## A (주체 설정) - Subject Deep Dive

### The Power of Context (맥락)

A is not just WHO, but WHO + CONTEXT.

**Examples of weak A:**
- A doctor
- A chef
- An athlete
- A teacher

**Examples of strong A:**
- A doctor **who worked 20 years in rural clinics**
- A chef **who cooks for a Michelin 3-star restaurant**
- An athlete **who came back from career-ending injury**
- A teacher **who taught in 5 different countries**

### Types of Context

**1. Experience Context**
- Years in field
- Unique background
- Specialization

Example: "30-year sommelier" vs "sommelier"

**2. Positional Context**
- Role or status
- Access to information
- Perspective

Example: "Former professional athlete" vs "fitness enthusiast"

**3. Situational Context**
- Current circumstance
- Challenge or constraint
- Transformation

Example: "Former CEO who became a monk" vs "monk"

### A-Building Exercise

Take a generic subject and add context:

**Generic**: A programmer
**Add experience**: A programmer with 10 years at Google
**Add situation**: A programmer with 10 years at Google who quit to become a farmer
**Result**: Now viewers are curious WHY

**Generic**: A parent
**Add situation**: A parent of triplets
**Add experience**: A parent of triplets who works night shifts
**Result**: Specific empathy point

### Common A Mistakes

❌ **Too vague**: "A person"
→ No hook, no reason to care

❌ **Too niche**: "A left-handed underwater basket weaver in Slovenia"
→ So specific that no one can relate

❌ **Credential dumping**: "A PhD Harvard-educated former Olympic athlete..."
→ Too much, feels like résumé not person

✅ **Just right**: "A Harvard professor who failed out of high school"
→ Surprising contrast, relatable struggle

## B (행위 설정) - Action Deep Dive

### The Power of Constraints (특별함)

B is not just WHAT they're doing, but WHAT + CONSTRAINT.

**Examples of weak B:**
- Cooks food
- Gives advice
- Explains something
- Shows their day

**Examples of strong B:**
- Cooks **5-star restaurant meal with gas station ingredients**
- Gives advice **in 30 seconds with no jargon**
- Explains **quantum physics using only children's toys**
- Shows their day **on $10 budget in NYC**

### Types of Constraints

**1. Resource Constraints**
- Time limit
- Budget limit
- Material limit

Example: "Makes wedding dress from thrift store clothes under $50"

**2. Challenge Constraints**
- Unusual method
- Reversed approach
- Difficulty level

Example: "Professional chef tries to recreate McDonald's exactly"

**3. Comparison Constraints**
- Cheap vs expensive
- Real vs fake
- Before vs after

Example: "Tests $10 vs $1000 headphones"

### A-B Dynamics

The relationship between A and B creates the hook:

**Reinforcing (A enhances B)**
- A: Professional race car driver
- B: Teaches defensive driving to teenagers
- Dynamic: Expertise makes basic task more interesting

**Contrasting (A clashes with B)**
- A: Minimalist lifestyle expert
- B: Spends $10,000 in one day
- Dynamic: Contradiction creates curiosity

**Revealing (B shows hidden side of A)**
- A: Tough drill sergeant
- B: Talks about his skincare routine
- Dynamic: Unexpected vulnerability

### B-Building Exercise

Take a basic action and add constraint:

**Basic**: Reviews products
**Add challenge**: Reviews products by only reading instructions, never using them
**Add resource**: Reviews products from 1990s and compares to modern versions
**Result**: Clear angle that makes reviews interesting

**Basic**: Teaches language
**Add time**: Teaches language in 10-minute sprints
**Add method**: Teaches language using only movie scenes
**Result**: Specific promise to viewer

### Common B Mistakes

❌ **No constraint**: "A chef cooks"
→ So what? That's their job

❌ **Fake constraint**: "A doctor tries being a doctor!"
→ That's just them doing their job

❌ **Impossible constraint**: "A lawyer argues case in ancient Sanskrit"
→ So outlandish it's not interesting, just weird

✅ **Just right**: "A lawyer explains famous court cases in simple English"
→ Useful constraint that serves audience

## C (감상/이유) - Payoff Deep Dive

### Why "So What?" Matters

C is the hardest to define but most important. It's WHY anyone should invest their time.

**C is NOT:**
- "It's informative"
- "It's entertaining"
- "It's educational"

These are too generic.

**C IS:**
- A specific emotion
- A clear insight
- A useful takeaway
- A shared feeling

### Types of C (Emotional Payoffs)

**1. Validation**
"I knew it! My instincts were right!"

Example:
- A: Luxury salesperson
- B: Explains tricks high-end stores use
- C: Viewers feel validated about feeling manipulated

**2. Discovery**
"Whoa, I never thought about it that way!"

Example:
- A: Former prison guard
- B: Explains why jail food is designed that way
- C: Viewers gain unexpected insight into system design

**3. Aspiration**
"I want to be like that / achieve that!"

Example:
- A: 60-year-old bodybuilder
- B: Shows morning routine
- C: Viewers feel inspired about aging

**4. Schadenfreude**
"Ha! I'm glad I'm not the only one!"

Example:
- A: Professional organizer
- B: Visits home of a maximalist artist
- C: Viewers feel less bad about their own mess

**5. Justice/Catharsis**
"Finally, someone said it!"

Example:
- A: Restaurant health inspector
- B: Reviews viral "clean" restaurants
- C: Viewers feel vindicated about hygiene concerns

**6. Practical Value**
"I can actually use this!"

Example:
- A: Former barista
- B: Shows how to make café drinks at home
- C: Viewers save money and learn skill

### C-Building Exercise

Ask "그래서 뭐?" (So what?) three times:

**Example:**
- A: Professional organizer
- B: Organizes a hoarder's home
- So what? → They make the space clean
- So what? → The person can live better
- So what? → **It shows transformation is possible even in extreme cases**
- C: Hope for anyone feeling overwhelmed

**Example:**
- A: Cybersecurity expert
- B: Hacks into his own home devices
- So what? → He shows vulnerabilities
- So what? → People learn their devices aren't safe
- So what? → **Viewers feel validated about their paranoia and empowered to protect themselves**
- C: Validation + empowerment

### Common C Mistakes

❌ **No clear emotion**: "It's interesting"
→ What emotion? Curiosity? Fear? Joy?

❌ **Too broad**: "People will learn something"
→ Learn what specifically?

❌ **Contradicts A-B**: 
- A-B sets up comedy, C tries to be serious
- A-B sets up education, C tries to be funny

✅ **Just right**: "Feel relieved that experts struggle too"
→ Specific emotion, clear takeaway

## A-B-C Integration

### Testing Your A-B-C

Once you have all three, test against these questions:

**1. Can you explain it in one sentence?**
"A [A with context] who [B with constraint] to show [C payoff]"

Example: "A former teacher who grades famous speeches like homework to show how even 'perfect' communication has flaws"

**2. Would you watch this?**
Be honest. If YOU wouldn't watch, fix it.

**3. Can you pitch it without looking at notes?**
If you can't remember your own concept, it's not clear enough.

**4. Does it surprise you slightly?**
Good A-B-C combinations have a "huh, interesting" factor.

**5. Can you imagine the thumbnail?**
If you can't visualize it, B might be too abstract.

### Fixing Broken A-B-C

**Problem: Concept feels flat**

Check:
- Are both A and B boring? → Add context to A or constraint to B
- Are both A and B wild? → Ground one of them
- Is C unclear? → Do the "So what?" exercise

**Problem: Can't identify target audience**

Check:
- Is A too niche? → Broaden the context
- Is B too inside-baseball? → Make constraint more universal
- Is C too personal? → Find the shared emotion

**Problem: Feels like clickbait**

Check:
- Does A-B promise something C doesn't deliver? → Realign
- Is B a fake constraint? → Make it genuine
- Does C feel manipulative? → Connect to authentic emotion

## Real Examples from Creatorhood

### Example 1: CH MAG Fitness Series

**First attempt (weak):**
- A: Gym trainer
- B: Shows exercises
- C: Get fit

**Problem**: All generic. Could be any fitness content.

**Refined:**
- A: Former Olympic trainer (context: elite credentials)
- B: Demonstrates apartment workouts with no equipment in 5 minutes (constraint: time + resources)
- C: Get Olympic-level advice that actually works in real life (payoff: expertise made accessible)

**One-line**: "Former Olympic trainer shows 5-minute no-equipment apartment workouts"

### Example 2: Brand Content (Kuksundang)

**First attempt (weak):**
- A: Makgeolli expert
- B: Talks about makgeolli
- C: Learn about makgeolli

**Problem**: Educational but not compelling.

**Refined:**
- A: Third-generation brewery owner (context: heritage)
- B: Explains why cheap vs expensive makgeolli taste different (constraint: comparison)
- C: Understand what you're actually paying for (payoff: informed choice)

**One-line**: "Brewery owner reveals the truth about makgeolli pricing"

### Example 3: CH MAG Lifestyle Series

**First attempt (weak):**
- A: Runner
- B: Shows running gear
- C: Buy good gear

**Problem**: Sounds like shopping content.

**Refined:**
- A: Ultra-marathon runner (context: extreme experience)
- B: Tests "$10 vs $200" running shoes for 100km (constraint: comparison + distance)
- C: Find out if expensive gear actually matters (payoff: informed decision)

**One-line**: "Ultra-runner tests cheap vs expensive shoes for 100km"

## Advanced: Series Development

Once you have a working A-B-C, you can scale it into a series by varying ONE element:

**Vary A (Different subjects, same format)**
- Keep: B (the constraint/format)
- Change: A (different experts/people)
- Example: "Different ultra-runners test cheap vs expensive gear"

**Vary B (Same subject, different scenarios)**
- Keep: A (same person/expertise)
- Change: B (different constraints)
- Example: "Ultra-runner tests shoes, then tests hydration packs, then tests nutrition"

**Vary C (Same A-B, different audiences/angles)**
- Keep: A-B (same person, same action)
- Change: C (different takeaway)
- Example: One video for beginners (feasibility), one for experts (optimization)

---

## Conclusion

The A-B-C Framework is simple but not easy:
- A requires specificity
- B requires creativity
- C requires empathy

Master these three elements, and you can plan compelling content consistently.

Remember: The best content makes the extraordinary accessible or makes the ordinary extraordinary. A-B-C is your tool to do both.
