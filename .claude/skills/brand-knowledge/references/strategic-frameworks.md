# Strategic Frameworks

Reusable strategic playbooks proven through case studies. Apply these frameworks to new clients by adapting to their specific context.

---

## Framework 1: Magazine Model vs. Performance Model

**When to use:** Aligning expectations with clients, especially e-commerce brands

### The Problem

Many brands approach social content with "magazine thinking":
- Focus on aesthetics and volume
- Hope more content = more results
- Can't connect content to business outcomes
- Long time to ROI, hard to justify investment

### The Framework

#### Magazine Model (Old Way)

**Characteristics:**
- High-volume posting (15-30+ posts/month)
- Aesthetic-first approach
- Lifestyle imagery and editorial style
- Fixed monthly fees
- KPIs: Followers, engagement rate, reach

**When it works:**
- Pure brand awareness plays
- Luxury brands where aspiration is the goal
- Long-term brand building (1-2+ years)
- Clients with patient capital

**When it fails:**
- E-commerce needing sales
- Startups needing fast ROI
- Brands with limited budgets
- Performance marketing mindset clients

**Our learning:** Magazine model works for CH MAG (our own brand building) but not always for client work.

#### Performance Model (New Way)

**Characteristics:**
- Conversion-focused content
- Every piece has business goal
- Direct attribution to outcomes
- Revenue share or performance bonuses
- KPIs: Sales, CAC, ROAS, conversion rate

**When it works:**
- E-commerce with online checkout
- Products ₩30K-200K price range
- Brands with existing traffic
- Clients wanting measurable ROI

**When it fails:**
- Very long sales cycles (B2B)
- Offline-only attribution
- Pure brand awareness goals
- Luxury high-consideration items

### How to Apply

**Step 1: Diagnose Client Need**
Ask:
- What's your primary business goal? (awareness vs. sales)
- How do you currently measure marketing success?
- What's your typical customer journey length?
- Do you have e-commerce with tracking capability?

**Step 2: Recommend Model**
- E-commerce with short journey → Performance Model
- Brand building or offline business → Magazine Model (modified)
- Hybrid situations → Hybrid approach

**Step 3: Set Expectations**
Magazine Model: "We'll build long-term brand asset, expect 6-12 months to see major impact"
Performance Model: "We'll drive measurable sales, expect 60-90 days for attribution data, iterate fast"

**Step 4: Structure Agreement**
Magazine: Fixed monthly fee, brand metrics
Performance: Revenue share or hybrid, sales metrics
Hybrid: Base fee + performance bonuses

### Case Study Applications

- **Kuksundang**: Magazine approach (brand building for traditional product)
- **Inhaze**: Performance approach (e-commerce conversion focus)
- **Pelicana**: Magazine approach (repositioning and content studio)

## Framework 2: Heritage Repositioning

**When to use:** Legacy brands (15-30+ years) struggling with "old" perception

### The Problem

Brands with history often see it as weakness:
- Newer competitors seem trendier
- Younger demos don't relate
- "Old brand" perception in market
- Scared to emphasize heritage

### The Framework

#### Step 1: Audit the Heritage Asset

**Questions to ask:**
- How many years in business?
- What era did you launch/peak? (80s, 90s, 2000s)
- What cultural moments were you part of?
- What authentic stories do you have?
- What can't new brands claim?

**Look for:**
- Specific year markers (25 years, 30 years)
- Cultural era connections (Y2K, 90s, etc.)
- Craftsmanship or traditional methods
- Generational customer stories
- Original locations or recipes

**Example: Pelicana**
- 26 years (launched 1998)
- Y2K era brand
- Part of Korean family moments
- Consistent recipe/quality

#### Step 2: Find Contemporary Lens

**Don't just say "we're old"**

**Find the angle:**

**Option A: Retro/Nostalgia**
- Best for: 90s-2000s brands
- Appeal: MZ generation loves retro they didn't fully experience
- Execution: Y2K aesthetics, vintage filters, nostalgic music
- Example: Pelicana "26년 → 레트로"

**Option B: Craft/Documentary**
- Best for: Traditional production methods
- Appeal: Authenticity in age of mass production
- Execution: Behind-the-scenes, maker stories, process
- Example: Kuksundang "막걸리 현장"

**Option C: Cultural Archive**
- Best for: Brands central to cultural moments
- Appeal: Part of shared memory and history
- Execution: Archive content, then-and-now, generational
- Example: Pelicana "26년 아카이브" series

**Option D: Counter-Trend**
- Best for: When everyone else is new
- Appeal: "Things that last" vs. fly-by-night
- Execution: Stability, reliability, proven
- Positioning: "Trends come and go, we stay"

#### Step 3: Visual System Development

**Create distinctive look that bridges eras:**

**For Retro Approach:**
- 90s-Y2K design elements
- Vintage filters, film grain
- Period typography
- Nostalgic color palettes
- BUT executed with contemporary polish

**For Craft/Documentary:**
- Natural, authentic aesthetics
- Behind-the-scenes feel
- Film photography look
- Minimal production gloss
- Focus on hands, process, materials

**For Cultural Archive:**
- Mix old and new
- Remastered vintage content
- Side-by-side comparisons
- Timeline narratives
- Modern context for old stories

#### Step 4: Content Series Structure

**Don't do one-off posts**

**Create series around heritage:**

**Series Type 1: "Then & Now"**
- Compare original to current
- Show evolution while maintaining essence
- Example: "1998 first store vs. 2024"

**Series Type 2: "The [X] Year Archive"**
- Weekly/monthly heritage moments
- Remastered old ads or content
- Stories from the years
- Example: Pelicana "26년 아카이브"

**Series Type 3: "How We Still..."**
- Traditional methods maintained
- Craftsmanship unchanged
- Quality consistency
- Example: Kuksundang brewing process

**Series Type 4: "Generations"**
- Multi-generational customer stories
- Family traditions with brand
- Passed-down favorites
- Example: "Three generations of Pelicana"

#### Step 5: Target Audience Bridge

**Don't abandon existing customers**

**Strategy:**
- Primary Content: Aimed at younger demos (MZ)
- With elements that resonate with existing (30-40s+)
- Use positioning that works for both

**Example - Retro:**
- MZ sees: Cool, trendy, aesthetic
- 30-40s see: Authentic nostalgia, "my era"
- Both: Quality, consistency, not trying too hard

**Example - Documentary:**
- MZ sees: Authentic, not advertising, discovery
- 30-40s see: Respect for tradition, quality maintained
- Both: Appreciation for craft and story

### How to Apply

**Client Analysis:**
```
Brand: [Name]
Years: [X years]
Era: [80s/90s/2000s]
Current perception: [old/outdated/legacy]
Desired perception: [authentic/iconic/cool]

Heritage assets:
- [Specific years or milestones]
- [Cultural moments or connections]
- [Traditional methods or quality]
- [Stories new brands don't have]

Recommended lens: [Retro/Documentary/Archive/Counter-trend]
Why: [Reasoning based on era and brand]

Visual direction: [Specific aesthetic system]
Content series: [3 series concepts]
Target bridge: [How it appeals to both demographics]
```

### Success Metrics

**6-month goals:**
- Sentiment shift in comments (old → cool/authentic)
- Younger demographic growth in followers
- UGC using your heritage theme
- Media mentions connecting brand to positioning
- Maintain or grow existing customer engagement

### Case Study Applications

- **Pelicana**: 26 years through retro lens, Y2K aesthetic, film cinematics
- **Kuksundang**: Traditional through documentary scenes, authentic storytelling

## Framework 3: Scene-Based Strategy (막걸리 현장 Model)

**When to use:** Brands wanting authentic, non-advertising content; F&B, lifestyle, cultural products

### The Problem

Most brand content feels like advertising:
- "Buy our product"
- Feature-benefit lists
- Staged, perfect scenarios
- Obviously promotional

Audiences are ad-fatigued and skeptical.

### The Framework

#### Core Principle: Show Scenes, Not Ads

**The Concept:**
Instead of promoting product, show scenes/moments where product naturally exists.

**Key shift:**
- FROM: "Here's our product and why it's great"
- TO: "Here's a moment, and our product happens to be there"

**Inspired by:** Kuksundang "막걸리 현장" strategy

#### The Three Scene Types

**Scene Type 1: MAKING (Production)**
- What it shows: How product is made, behind-the-scenes
- Why people watch: Curiosity, appreciation for craft
- Content approach: Documentary observation, minimal explanation
- Example: Brewing process, seasonal variations, maker stories

**Scene Type 2: CONSUMING (Usage)**
- What it shows: People using/enjoying product in real contexts
- Why people watch: Relatable moments, social connection
- Content approach: Conversations, atmosphere, genuine enjoyment
- Example: Friends over drinks, family meal, special occasions

**Scene Type 3: DISCOVERING (Outsider)**
- What it shows: Fresh perspective, learning, cultural bridge
- Why people watch: Vicarious discovery, education through story
- Content approach: Outsider POV, questions, comparisons
- Example: Foreigner trying for first time, expert analyzing

#### Step-by-Step Application

**Step 1: Identify Your Scenes**

Map where your product naturally exists:
- Making: Where/how is it produced? Who makes it? What's the process?
- Consuming: Where/when do people use it? What contexts? What moments?
- Discovering: Who's encountering it fresh? What questions do they have?

**Example - Coffee Brand:**
- Making: Roasting process, bean selection, barista training
- Consuming: Morning routine, study sessions, friend catch-ups
- Discovering: Tea drinker's first specialty coffee, coffee novice learning

**Step 2: Choose 3 Series**

Pick one from each type or weight toward what's most compelling:

**Format:**
- 30-60 sec documentary-style shorts
- Minimal narration, let scene speak
- Real people, real moments
- Authentic sound important

**Example - Traditional food brand:**
- Series 1: "The Kitchen" (Making) - How each dish is prepared
- Series 2: "The Table" (Consuming) - Families/friends sharing meals
- Series 3: "First Bite" (Discovering) - Non-Koreans trying traditional food

**Step 3: Establish Guidelines**

**DO:**
✓ Let scenes breathe, don't rush
✓ Use natural lighting and sound
✓ Feature real people with real reactions
✓ Allow imperfection and spontaneity
✓ Focus on moments, not products
✓ Minimal text overlay or explanation

**DON'T:**
✗ Stage everything perfectly
✗ Use advertising language
✗ Make product the hero
✗ Over-explain or educate
✗ Polish away all authenticity
✗ Create one-offs vs. series

**Step 4: Abstraction Check**

Before publishing, ask:
- Could this work WITHOUT showing the product? (Should be YES)
- Does it feel like an ad? (Should be NO)
- Would I watch this for the story itself? (Should be YES)
- Is the product naturally present, not forced? (Should be YES)

**The gold standard:**
Viewer discovers brand organically through compelling scene, not sold to directly.

### Content Production Approach

**Documentary Style:**
- Observational camera work
- Longer takes (not TikTok pace)
- Natural sound and ambient audio
- Minimal music or subtle background
- Let moments develop naturally

**Editing:**
- Less cuts = more authentic feel
- Don't over-produce
- Keep human pauses and reactions
- Build atmosphere, not just information

**Tone:**
- Curious, not promotional
- Respectful, not exploitative
- Warm, not clinical
- Inviting, not pushy

### How to Apply

**Client Brief:**
```
Brand: [Name]
Product: [What you sell]
Current approach: [Typical ad-style? Feature lists?]
Desired feel: [Authentic, documentary, scene-based]

Scene mapping:
MAKING scenes:
- [Where product is made/prepared]
- [Who makes it, their stories]
- [Process that's interesting]

CONSUMING scenes:
- [Where people use it]
- [What moments or occasions]
- [Who uses it, in what context]

DISCOVERING scenes:
- [Who's encountering it fresh]
- [What questions do they have]
- [What's surprising or interesting]

Recommended 3 series:
1. [Title] - [Making/Consuming/Discovering] - [Brief concept]
2. [Title] - [Type] - [Concept]
3. [Title] - [Type] - [Concept]

Production approach: [Documentary guidelines specific to brand]
```

### Success Metrics

**Qualitative:**
- Comments focus on story, not just product
- Shares (people want others to see the scene)
- Saves (content has value to revisit)
- User-generated scenes with brand

**Quantitative:**
- Higher completion rates (people watch to end)
- Lower skip rates
- Organic reach (algorithm favors authentic)
- Time on profile increased

### Case Study Application

**Kuksundang:**
- 순당포착 (Making): Brewing documentary
- Mic Drop (Consuming): Conversations over makgeolli
- 외국인이 막걸리를 (Discovering): Foreign perspectives

Result: Brand felt authentic and cultural, not advertising

## Framework 4: Retro Visual System

**When to use:** Brands from 90s-2000s targeting MZ generation; need distinctive aesthetic

### The Problem

- Brand from 90s-2000s era
- Visual identity feels dated or generic
- Need contemporary relevance
- MZ audience has Y2K nostalgia
- Want distinctive, recognizable look

### The Framework

#### The Retro Opportunity

**Why retro works now:**
- MZ generation nostalgic for era they didn't fully live
- Y2K aesthetic major trend (fashion, music, design)
- 90s "authenticity" appeals vs. overly polished today
- Creates instant visual differentiation

**When to use:**
- Brands established 1990-2005
- Categories with cultural nostalgia (food, fashion, entertainment)
- Targeting 20-35 year olds primarily
- Want warm, accessible, not luxury cold

#### Step 1: Era Identification

**Pinpoint your specific era:**

**Early 90s (1990-1995)**
- Analog warmth
- Pre-digital aesthetic
- Bold colors, geometric shapes
- Example: Sega, early MTV

**Late 90s (1996-1999)**
- Digital emergence
- Y2K approaching
- Futuristic optimism
- Example: iMac, Backstreet Boys

**Early 2000s (2000-2005)**
- Y2K passed, tech boom
- Early digital cameras
- Flip phones, early internet
- Example: iPod, early reality TV

**Brand founded:** [Year]
**Peak cultural moment:** [Year range]
**Best retro era match:** [Specific period]

#### Step 2: Visual Elements System

**Design Language:**

**Typography**
- Chunky, bold fonts (90s)
- Pixel fonts (early digital)
- Hand-drawn or brush fonts (analog feel)
- Mix Korean and English
- Imperfect alignment (not rigid grid)

**Color Palette**
- Primary: Keep brand colors
- Secondary: Retro pastels (mint, peach, lavender)
- Accent: Hot pink, electric blue, lime (Y2K pops)
- Treatment: Slight desaturation, film-like

**Layout**
- Asymmetric, playful compositions
- Collage aesthetics
- Layered textures
- Generous negative space
- Analog imperfections (cut edges, tape marks)

**Textures**
- Film grain overlays
- Paper textures (magazine/print feel)
- VHS scan lines (subtle, not overused)
- Halftone dots (newspaper reference)
- Analog artifacts

**Photography Style**
- Film camera aesthetic (not digital perfect)
- Warm color grading
- Slight vignetting
- Natural grain
- Candid, not overly posed

#### Step 3: Video Aesthetics

**[Film Look Cinematics]**

**Camera Work:**
- Handheld but intentional (not gimbal smooth)
- Fixed lens aesthetic (not zoom)
- Natural light preferred
- Golden hour warmth
- Close-ups and medium shots

**Grading:**
- 16mm or Super 8 film emulation
- Warm color temperature
- Organic grain (not digital noise)
- Slight highlight bloom
- Gentle vignette

**Editing:**
- Longer takes (let scenes breathe)
- Film-style cuts (not TikTok rapid)
- Minimal transitions (straight cuts)
- Authentic pace, not hyper-speed

**Sound Design:**
- Ambient sound important
- Period-appropriate music
- Vinyl crackle on audio (very subtle)
- Natural reverb
- No overly digital effects

#### Step 4: Content Applications

**Apply retro system across:**

**Static Posts:**
- Product shots with retro props (Discman, film camera, etc.)
- Collage-style layouts
- Retro typography treatments
- Analog texture overlays

**Video Content:**
- Film look shortform (Reels)
- VHS-style archive content
- Retro transitions (film flares, light leaks)
- Period music choices

**Stories:**
- Retro sticker sets
- Analog frame templates
- Vintage-style polls
- Throwback music stickers

**Highlights:**
- Retro icon covers
- Consistent retro framing
- Period-appropriate imagery

#### Step 5: Consistency Guidelines

**Retro system must be:**

**Consistent (not random)**
- Every piece follows visual rules
- Recognizable as brand instantly
- System, not sporadic retro posts

**Refined (not kitschy)**
- Subtle retro cues, not costume party
- Contemporary execution of retro elements
- Sophisticated, not cheap throwback

**Authentic (not trying too hard)**
- Brand actually from this era
- Organic connection, not forced trend
- Respectful nostalgia, not mocking

**Flexible (not rigid)**
- Allow variation within system
- Adapt to content needs
- Evolve over time

#### Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
✓ Use retro as aesthetic filter
✓ Mix analog warmth with digital polish
✓ Reference specific era authentically
✓ Make it feel timeless, not dated
✓ Let retro enhance story, not be the story

**DON'T:**
✗ Overuse retro to point of parody
✗ Mix incompatible era references
✗ Make it look cheap or low-budget
✗ Force retro into every single piece
✗ Lose brand recognition in nostalgia

### How to Apply

**Brand Retro Brief:**
```

Founded: [Year]
Era match: [Early 90s / Late 90s / Early 2000s]
Why: [Connection to that specific period]

- Typography: [Specific font directions]
- Colors: [Palette with hex codes]
- Textures: [Which textures to use]
- Photo style: [Film type, grading notes]
- Video approach: [Specific references]

Content applications:
- Static posts: [How to apply system]
- Video content: [Film look specifics]
- Stories: [Retro elements]

Consistency rules:
- [What must be consistent]
- [What can vary]
- [Quality standards]

References:
- [Visual examples, mood board]
- [Era-specific cultural references]
```

### Success Metrics

**Visual recognition:**
- Can people identify brand from aesthetic alone?
- Scroll-stopping quality (people pause)
- Save rate (aesthetic value)

**Target resonance:**
- MZ comments about retro/nostalgia
- 30-40s comments about authenticity
- UGC mimicking aesthetic

**Differentiation:**
- Visually distinct from competitors
- Media mentions of visual identity
- Other brands copying approach (ultimate proof)

### Case Study Application

**Pelicana:**
- 26-year heritage from 1998 (peak Y2K)
- Retro aesthetic system (90s-Y2K)
- Film look cinematics for video
- Consistent visual recognition
- "페리카나 = retro" keyword ownership

## Quick Framework Selection Guide

**Client says:** "We need to prove ROI" → **Framework 1: Performance Model**

**Client says:** "We're seen as old/outdated" → **Framework 2: Heritage Repositioning**

**Client says:** "We want authentic, not ad-like content" → **Framework 3: Scene-Based Strategy**

**Client is:** 90s-2000s brand targeting MZ → **Framework 4: Retro Visual System**

**Client needs:** Multiple frameworks can be combined!
Example: Pelicana used Framework 2 (Heritage) + Framework 4 (Retro) + Framework 3 (Scene-based moments)

---

**Remember:** These aren't rigid rules. They're starting points. Adapt to client's specific context, category, audience, and goals. The best strategies often combine elements from multiple frameworks.
